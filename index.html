    Ans: The first graph in the dashboard, on the top left corner, displays the number of international games per year (Top 20 in the selected range). The range starts out as 1970-2020, but is available for the entire timeline of games that the dataset has (1872-2020).
    The second graph on the right side of the page, is a Choropleth, which is a world map colored based on the win percentage of the teams, the darkest shade of the color blue representing the maximum win percentage and the lightest shade representing the least. These probabilities, as mentioned under the graph title are calculate based on the winning percentage, goals scored, and the goals conceded in the past two world-cups. The reason why I decided to include a combination of these factors is that the winning percentage takes into account ratio between the number of games won and the total number of games played. If we take its product with the ratio of the total number of goal scored, and the total number of goals conceded, we will get a waited product of the win ratios, based on how strong those victories were (if goals scored are greater goals conceded the win percentage is amplified), and therefore probabilities calculated from that will allow us to judge a winner in a better way. The reason why I do not take into account the difficulty of the opponent is because of how unpredictable international football and the world cup is, and therefore, I gave all teams a real chance of doing well.

  • Reflect on the stages of your design and implementation process when you could have taken steps to make your dashboard more accessible to all audiences. What are some factors that kept you from taking these steps? (a few sentences)
    Ans: There were a bunch of stages during the implementation process where I did or should have thought about accessibility. The first one would be the structure of the page itself. A clearly defined heading structure makes it easy for blind readers to understand the context better while using a screen reader. A simple assumption would be that the headings at the top would be larger than the headings below so I kept it that way. Other than that I defined roles for elements too, which would also help with context, telling the user what the role of a certain element is. Moreover, when adding the slider, I had to make a decision to give the input a form label, so that the screen reader knows what the input is about, and I also had to give the slider ARIA labels, so the reader knows where it starts, where it ends, what's the year range etc. What I could not do, was structure the svg's in a way that they are all tabbable or more explicitly, the elements in the svg could all be scrolled using the keyboard. This was hard because of the fact that I was new to working with svg and hence was not able to incorporate this, which would be an essential part for people who cannot use a mouse, because this would allow them to understand all the visualizations just with the keyboard, but I will sure keep this in mind and practice for future implementations.
